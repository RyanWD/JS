The Three Laws of Robotics

  1. A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.

  2. A robot must obey the orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.

  3. A robot must protect its own existence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Laws.

Awards and recognition

Asimov won more than a dozen annual awards for particular works of science fiction and a half dozen lifetime awards. He also received 14 honorary doctorate degrees from universities.

  • 1957 – Thomas Alva Edison Foundation Award for best science book for youth, for Building Blocks of the Universe[149]

  • 1960 – Howard W. Blakeslee Award from the American Heart Association for The Living River[150]

  • 1962 – Boston University's Publication Merit Award[151]

  • 1963 – A special Hugo Award for "adding science to science fiction," for essays published in the Magazine of Fantasy and Science Fiction[112]

  • 1963 – Fellow of the American Academy of Arts and Sciences[152]

  • 1964 – The Science Fiction Writers of America voted "Nightfall" (1941) the all-time best science fiction short story[67]

  • 1965 – James T. Grady Award of the American Chemical Society (now called the James T. Grady-James H. Stack Award for Interpreting Chemistry)[153]

  • 1966 – Best All-time Novel Series Hugo Award for the Foundation series[154]

  • 1967 – Edward E. Smith Memorial Award[155]

  • 1967 – AAAS-Westinghouse Science Writing Award for Magazine Writing, for essay Over the Edge of the Universe (in the March 1967 Harper's Magazine)[156]

  • 1972 – Nebula Award for Best Novel for The Gods Themselves[157]

  • 1973 – Hugo Award for Best Novel for The Gods Themselves[157]

  • 1973 – Locus Award for Best Novel for The Gods Themselves[157]

  • 1975 – Klumpke-Roberts Award "for outstanding contributions to the public understanding and appreciation of astronomy"[158]

  • 1977 – Hugo Award for Best Novelette for The Bicentennial Man[159]

  • 1977 – Nebula Award for Best Novelette for The Bicentennial Man[160]

  • 1981 – An asteroid, 5020 Asimov, was named in his honor[10]

  • 1983 – Hugo Award for Best Novel for Foundation's Edge[161]

  • 1983 – Locus Award for Best Science Fiction Novel for Foundation's Edge[161]

  • 1986 – The Science Fiction and Fantasy Writers of America named him its 8th SFWA Grand Master (presented in 1987).[162]

  • 1987 – Locus Award for Best Short Story for "Robot Dreams"[163]

  • 1992 – Hugo Award for Best Novelette for Gold[164]

  • 1995 – Hugo Award for Best Non-Fiction Book for I. Asimov: A Memoir[165]

  • 1996 – A 1946 Retro-Hugo for Best Novel of 1945 was given at the 1996 WorldCon to "The Mule", the 7th Foundation story, published in Astounding Science Fiction[166]

  • 1997 – The Science Fiction and Fantasy Hall of Fame inducted Asimov in its second class of two deceased and two living persons, along with H. G. Wells and following editors Hugo Gernsback and John W. Campbell.[167]

  • 2000 – Asimov was featured on a stamp in Israel[168]

  • 2009 – A crater on the planet Mars, Asimov,[11] was named in his honor

  • 2010 – In the US Congress bill about the designation of the National Robotics Week as an annual event, a tribute to Isaac Asimov is as follows: "Whereas the second week in April each year is designated as `National Robotics Week', recognizing the accomplishments of Isaac Asimov, who immigrated to America, taught science, wrote science books for children and adults, first used the term robotics, developed the Three Laws of Robotics, and died in April, 1992: Now, therefore, be it resolved..."[169]

  • 2015 – Selected as a member of the New York State Writers Hall of Fame.[170]

  • 2016 – A 1941 Retro-Hugo for Best Short Story of 1940 was given at the 2016 WorldCon to Robbie, his first positronic robot story, published in Super Science Stories, September 1940[171]

The Grand Master of science fiction and great popularizer of science, Isaac Asimov was born in Russia in 1920 and grew up in the USA. A remarkable memory helped him finish high school before he was sixteen. He gained his doctorate in chemistry in 1949. His first story was published in 1939 in the magazine Amazing stories and he went on to write nearly five hundred books and several hundred articles on various subjects. He won the Hugo Award four times and the Nebula Award once. Asimov also wrote highly successful detective mysteries, histories of science, encyclopaedias, textbooks and a two-volume autobiography. He died in 1992.
